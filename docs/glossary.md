This glossary provides definitions for key terms used throughout the "Physical AI and Humanoid Robots" course. It aims to cover approximately 50 terms, sorted alphabetically (A-Z), with concise 1-2 sentence definitions relevant to embodied AI, kinematics, SLAM, ROS 2, and other core robotics and AI concepts.

**Actuation**
: The process of converting a control signal into physical motion, typically by motors or hydraulic systems in robots. This is fundamental for enabling robot movement and interaction.

**AI (Artificial Intelligence)**
: The theory and development of computer systems able to perform tasks that normally require human intelligence, such as visual perception, speech recognition, decision-making, and translation between languages.

**Algorithm**
: A set of rules or instructions to be followed in calculations or other problem-solving operations, especially by a computer.

**Anthrobotics**
: The field concerned with the development of humanoid robots. It focuses on replicating human form and capabilities in artificial agents.

**Application Programming Interface (API)**
: A set of definitions and protocols for building and integrating application software. In robotics, APIs allow different software components to communicate.

**Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)**
: A hypothetical type of intelligent agent that possesses the intellectual capability of a human, enabling it to understand or learn any intellectual task that a human being can.

**Artificial Narrow Intelligence (ANI)**
: Also known as Weak AI, this AI is designed and trained for a particular task, such as virtual assistants or self-driving cars. Most AI today is ANI.

**Asimov's Laws of Robotics**
: A set of rules formulated by Isaac Asimov in science fiction, positing ethical constraints for robots: a robot may not injure a human or allow a human to come to harm; a robot must obey orders given to it by human beings; a robot must protect its own existence.

**Autonomous System**
: A system that can perform tasks or make decisions without human intervention. This is a key goal of advanced robotics and AI.

**Backpropagation**
: An algorithm used in artificial neural networks to adjust the weights and biases of the network based on the error in the output, enabling learning.

**Behavior Tree**
: A programming structure used in artificial intelligence, especially in games and robotics, to manage complex decision-making and sequences of actions.

**Bipedal Locomotion**
: The act of walking on two legs, a significant challenge in robotics requiring sophisticated balance and control systems.

**Boston Dynamics**
: A prominent robotics company known for its advanced robots with exceptional mobility and agility, such as Atlas.

**Boston Dynamics Atlas**
: An advanced humanoid robot developed by Boston Dynamics, renowned for its dynamic balancing, agility, and manipulation capabilities.

**Calibration**
: The process of adjusting or setting a measuring instrument or system to meet a required standard or to achieve the desired accuracy. In robotics, this applies to sensors and manipulators.

**Central Processing Unit (CPU)**
: The primary component of a computer that performs most of the processing required to run a computer. In robotics, it's the brain for computation.

**Cloud Robotics**
: A paradigm that leverages cloud computing for robot computation, storage, and data access, enabling robots to access vast resources and collaborate.

**Cognitive Architecture**
: A theoretical framework for the structure of an intelligent agent, often inspired by human cognition, aiming to replicate human-like reasoning and learning.

**Computer Vision**
: A field of artificial intelligence that enables computers and systems to derive meaningful information from digital images, videos, and other visual inputs.

**Control System**
: A system designed to manage, command, direct, or regulate the behavior of other devices or systems, crucial for robot movement and interaction.

**Data Set**
: A collection of data, typically organized in a table or array, used for training and evaluating machine learning models.

**Deep Learning**
: A subset of machine learning that uses artificial neural networks with multiple layers (deep neural networks) to learn from large amounts of data.

**Definition List**
: A Markdown list format consisting of terms and their corresponding definitions, typically using a colon (`:`) to separate them.

**Docusaurus**
: An open-source static site generator project by Meta (Facebook) used for building fast, responsive documentation websites.

**Distributed System**
: A system whose components are located on different networked computers, which communicate and coordinate their actions by passing messages. Common in robotics for modular control.

**Dynamic Programming**
: A method for solving complex problems by breaking them down into simpler subproblems and storing the results of subproblems to avoid recomputation. Used in optimization and AI.

**Dynamics**
: The study of motion considering the forces that cause it; in robotics, it involves the relationship between forces, torques, and the resulting motion of the robot.

**Embodied AI**
: Artificial intelligence integrated into physical embodiments (robots) that interact directly with the real world, requiring perception, action, and adaptation.

**End-Effector**
: The device at the end of a robotic arm or manipulator, designed to interact with the environment (e.g., gripper, welder, camera).

**Environment**
: The physical or simulated space in which a robot or AI agent operates and interacts.

**Error Handling**
: The process of anticipating, detecting, and resolving errors or exceptions that occur during program execution. Essential for robust systems.

**Ethics in AI/Robotics**
: The study and application of moral principles related to the development and deployment of artificial intelligence and robotic systems, addressing issues like bias, safety, and autonomy.

**Exoskeleton**
: A wearable mechanical structure that augments or assists the wearer's limbs, often incorporating sensors and actuators for enhanced strength or mobility.

**Feature Engineering**
: The process of using domain knowledge to extract features from raw data that make machine learning algorithms work better.

**Feedback Loop**
: A system where the output of a process is used as an input to influence or control that process, vital for robot control and learning.

**Forward Kinematics**
: The calculation of the position and orientation of the end-effector of a robot based on the joint angles.

**Gait**
: The pattern of movement of the limbs of a robot, particularly relevant for bipedal or legged robots, enabling locomotion.

**Generalization**
: In machine learning, the ability of a model to perform well on unseen data after being trained on a specific dataset.

**Git**
: A distributed version control system used for tracking changes in source code during software development. Essential for collaboration and code management.

**Hardware**
: The physical components of a computer system or robot, including processors, sensors, motors, and chassis.

**Hero Image**
: A large, prominent image or banner displayed at the top of a web page, often used to make a strong first impression.

**History of Humanoid Robots**
: The chronological development of robots designed to resemble the human body, from early concepts like Shakey to modern advanced systems like Boston Dynamics' Atlas and Tesla's Optimus.

**Humanoid Robot**
: A robot designed to resemble the human body in shape and movement, often with a torso, head, two arms, and two legs.

**Idempotency**
: In computing, an operation is idempotent if executing it multiple times produces the same result as executing it once. Important for API design.

**Industry Leaders**
: Companies and organizations at the forefront of innovation and development in a particular field, such as Tesla and Boston Dynamics in humanoid robotics.

**Inverse Kinematics**
: The calculation of the joint parameters required to place the end-effector of a robot at a desired position and orientation.

**Key Concepts**
: Fundamental ideas, principles, or theories within a field of study that are essential for understanding the subject matter.

**Kinematics**
: The study of motion without considering the forces that cause it; in robotics, it relates to the position, velocity, and acceleration of robot links and joints.

**Learning Outcomes**
: Specific, measurable achievements or skills a student is expected to gain upon successful completion of a course or module.

**Machine Learning (ML)**
: A subfield of AI that focuses on algorithms enabling systems to learn from data without being explicitly programmed.

**Markdown**
: A lightweight markup language with plain-text formatting syntax, widely used for creating formatted content on the web, including documentation.

**Markdown Definition List**
: A Markdown list format consisting of terms and their corresponding definitions, typically using a colon (`:`) to separate them.

**Mermaid**
: A JavaScript-based diagramming and charting tool that uses Markdown-inspired text definitions to create diagrams and flowcharts.

**Middleware**
: Software that acts as a bridge between an operating system or database and applications, often used in robotics for inter-process communication (e.g., ROS 2).

**Module**
: A distinct unit or section of a course, typically covering a specific theme or set of topics.

**Natural Language Processing (NLP)**
: A subfield of AI focused on enabling computers to understand, interpret, and generate human language.

**Node.js**
: A JavaScript runtime environment that allows developers to run JavaScript code outside of a web browser, commonly used for backend services and build tools.

**Observability**
: The ability to infer the internal state of a system by examining its external outputs (logs, metrics, traces). Crucial for debugging and monitoring.

**Perception**
: The ability of a robot or AI system to interpret sensory data (e.g., from cameras, lidar) to understand its environment.

**Physical AI**
: Artificial intelligence integrated into physical embodiments (robots) that interact directly with the real world, requiring perception, action, and adaptation.

**Plan.md**
: A document generated during the planning phase that outlines the technical approach, architecture, and dependencies for a feature.

**Priority**
: A ranking assigned to tasks or user stories indicating their importance or urgency in development.

**Prerequisites**
: Knowledge, skills, or background information expected of participants before starting a course or undertaking a task.

**Python**
: A high-level, interpreted, general-purpose programming language known for its readability and extensive libraries, widely used in AI and robotics.

**Quality Assurance (QA)**
: The process of ensuring that a product or service meets specified quality standards through systematic reviews and testing.

**ROS 2 (Robot Operating System)**
: A flexible framework for writing robot software. It is a next-generation robot system that enables developers to create robot applications.

**Refactor**
: The process of restructuring existing computer code without changing its external behavior, aimed at improving non-functional attributes like readability or maintainability.

**Reinforcement Learning (RL)**
: A type of machine learning where an agent learns to make a sequence of decisions by trying to maximize a reward it receives for its actions.

**Research.md**
: A document created during the planning phase to record findings from technical research, decisions made, and alternatives considered.

**Robotics**
: The interdisciplinary field concerning the design, construction, operation, and application of robots, as well as computer systems for their control, sensory feedback, and information processing.

**Scalability**
: The capability of a system, network, or process to handle a growing amount of work, or its potential to be enlarged to accommodate that growth.

**Scope**
: The defined boundaries and features of a project or task, outlining what is included and excluded.

**Security Best Practices**
: Guidelines and techniques implemented to protect systems, networks, and data from unauthorized access, damage, or theft.

**Semantic Versioning**
: A versioning scheme that assigns a version number to software based on its features, bug fixes, and breaking changes (MAJOR.MINOR.PATCH).

**Sensory Data**
: Information gathered by a robot's sensors (e.g., cameras, lidar, microphones) to perceive its environment.

**Service**
: A software component that performs a specific function and communicates with other components, often providing an API.

**Shakey the Robot**
: Considered one of the first mobile robots to reason about its actions, developed at SRI International in the late 1960s. A foundational step in AI and robotics.

**SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping)**
: A computational problem of constructing or updating a map of an unknown environment while simultaneously keeping track of an agent's location within it. Crucial for autonomous navigation.

**Software Engineering**
: The application of a systematic, disciplined, quantifiable approach to the development, operation, and maintenance of software.

**Specification (Spec)**
: A document detailing the requirements, features, and acceptance criteria for a piece of software or a project.

**Static Site Generator**
: A tool that generates an entire website as static HTML files from source files, simplifying deployment and improving performance (e.g., Docusaurus).

**Systematic Approach**
: A methodical, organized, and planned process for carrying out a task or solving a problem.

**TDD (Test-Driven Development)**
: A software development process where tests are written before the code, driving the design and implementation iteratively.

**Technical Debt**
: The implied cost of additional rework caused by choosing an easy (limited) solution now instead of using a better approach that would take longer.

**Template**
: A pre-designed structure or file that serves as a starting point for creating new documents or files, often containing placeholders.

**Testing**
: The process of evaluating a system or its component parts with the intent to find whether it satisfies the specified requirements.

**Thread**
: In software, a thread is the smallest sequence of programmed instructions that can be managed independently by a scheduler.

**Throughput**
: A measure of the rate at which a system can process requests or data over a given time period.

**Timeouts**
: A mechanism that sets a limit on the amount of time a process or operation can take before it is considered failed and is terminated.

**Traceability**
: The ability to track the history, location, or application of an item, often referring to requirements, tests, and code.

**Traditional AI**
: Refers to AI systems that operate primarily in digital or data-driven environments, lacking physical embodiment or direct real-world interaction capabilities.

**Trade-offs**
: In decision-making, the act of giving up one benefit or advantage in order to gain another regarded as more desirable.

**TDD (Test-Driven Development)**
: A software development process where tests are written before the code, driving the design and implementation iteratively.

**Unit Test**
: A type of software testing where individual units or components of a software are tested to verify they are suitable for use.

**User Story**
: A short, simple description of a feature told from the perspective of the person who desires the new capability, usually in the format "As a \[type of user], I want \[some goal] so that \[some reason]."

**Versioning**
: The process of managing changes to documents, code, or other artifacts over time, assigning specific versions to track changes and history.

**Virtual Environment**
: An isolated Python environment that allows you to manage dependencies for different projects separately.

**Visual Aids**
: Elements such as diagrams, charts, tables, or images used to enhance understanding and presentation of information.

**Void Function**
: A function that does not return any value.

**Web Application**
: A client-server software application in which the client (or user interface), learning, and data storage run in different places. The user interface runs in a web browser.

**Why Humanoids?**
: A section discussing the rationale, advantages, and unique challenges associated with designing robots in a human-like form.
